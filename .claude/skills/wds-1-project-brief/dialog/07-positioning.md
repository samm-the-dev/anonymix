# Positioning — Anonymix

## Conversation Summary

### Opening
Asked who would use this and why they'd pick it over alternatives.

### Key exchanges
- **Target:** Sam and improv friends. Fine if it stays small. Small friend groups who share music together.
- **Alternatives:** Music League is the primary alternative. Group has been using it for a while with no major complaints, some dropoffs. Shared playlists and group chats are the informal alternatives.
- **Pain points with ML:** Ads (confirmed multiple people dislike them), Spotify-only lock-out (Sam uses YTM), competition emphasis.
- **Differentiation:** Platform-agnostic (Spotify + YTM), discovery-first framing, no ads, no aggregate leaderboard.
- **Honest framing:** ML works well enough. This isn't solving a crisis — it's making a better version of something the group already enjoys.

## Positioning Components

| Component | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Target** | Small friend groups who enjoy themed music sharing |
| **Need** | Platform-inclusive, low-pressure music sharing with creative prompts |
| **Category** | Social music sharing game |
| **Key Benefit** | Everyone discovers new music through a fun, ad-free prompt format |
| **Alternatives** | Music League, shared Spotify/YTM playlists, group chat |
| **Differentiator** | Platform-agnostic (Spotify + YTM), discovery over competition, no ads |

## Positioning Statement (confirmed)

For small friend groups who enjoy themed music sharing, Anonymix is a social music game that turns prompts into shared playlists. Unlike Music League, it supports both Spotify and YouTube Music users, frames voting as resonance instead of ranking, and keeps the focus on discovery — no ads, no aggregate leaderboards, just new music to check out.
