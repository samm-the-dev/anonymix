# The Host — Priority 3 (TERTIARY)

> The organizer who keeps the game running

**Document:** Trigger Map - Tertiary Persona
**Created:** 2026-03-24
**Status:** COMPLETE

---

## Profile Summary

The Host is the person who actually creates the session, writes the prompts, sets the deadlines, and nudges people to participate. In most friend groups, this is one person — sometimes two. They're usually an Enthusiast who also happens to be the organizer type. They care about the game enough to do the admin work, but they need that admin work to be minimal or they'll burn out and the whole thing dies.

The Host is the sustainability layer. Without a Host, sessions don't start. Without easy hosting tools, the Host stops hosting.

---

## Who The Host Is

Same friend group, same music interests as The Enthusiast — but with an additional **organizer instinct**. They're the person who also plans the group trips, creates the group chats, suggests the restaurants. In the Music League context, they're the one who created the league and keeps creating rounds. They take quiet pride in keeping the thing going, but they also resent when it feels like work instead of fun.

---

## Psychological Profile

The Host values **facilitation and group momentum**. They get satisfaction from creating a prompt that sparks good submissions, from seeing participation numbers stay high, from the group having a shared activity that keeps them connected even when they don't see each other often.

They have a **caretaker dynamic with the group experience**. If participation drops, they feel responsible. If a round stalls, they feel the urge to nudge. This creates a subtle pressure: the game's health feels like their job, and if the tools make that job harder, they'll quietly resent it.

They need **just enough control without overhead**. Set a prompt, set a deadline, invite people — done. They don't want a dashboard with analytics. They don't want granular permissions. They want to create the container and let the group fill it.

Their relationship with the product is more **utilitarian** than The Enthusiast's. They care about the experience, but they also need the admin side to be clean. Clunky admin tools or confusing settings will make them dread creating the next round.

---

## Internal State

When creating a new tape, The Host feels **creative satisfaction** — writing a good prompt is its own small art form. When participation is high, they feel **quiet pride**. When people skip rounds or engagement drops, they feel **mild anxiety** — "is the group losing interest? Should I do something different?"

Their biggest emotional risk is **admin fatigue** — the feeling that maintaining the game is becoming a chore. If creating a tape requires too many steps, if managing members is awkward, if they can't easily see who's submitted, the fun drains out and they stop hosting.

---

## Usage Context

**Access/Discovery:** They're the one who found or built the thing. They set up the session initially and return regularly to create tapes and check status.

**Emotional State:** Purposeful when creating rounds. Satisfied when things run smoothly. Anxious when participation drops. Fatigued if admin is cumbersome.

**Behavior Pattern:** Creates tapes on a cadence (weekly or biweekly). Checks submission status to see who's in. Might nudge in group chat. Also participates as a player (submits, votes, comments). Reviews results with both player-brain and host-brain.

**Decision Criteria:** Can I create a tape in under a minute? Can I see at a glance who's submitted? Is inviting someone a single link? Can I transfer admin if I need a break?

**Success Outcome:** Tape created quickly, participation is healthy, the round runs itself once set up, and they get to enjoy playing too.

---

## Relationship to Business Goals

- ✅ **Active Participation (PRIMARY):** Without The Host, there are no tapes — and without tapes, there's no participation. They're the enabler.
- ✅ **Engagement Quality:** Good prompts from skilled hosts drive better submissions and more interesting discussions.
- ✅ **Organic Growth:** Host expansion IS a success metric. If others start hosting, the product's dependency on a single organizer drops.

---

## Driving Forces

### WANTS
- ✅ **Create rounds effortlessly** — write a prompt, set a deadline, and get back to being a player, not an admin (when starting a new tape)
- ✅ **See the group engaged** — glanceable status showing who's submitted and voted, confirming the round is alive (when checking tape progress)
- ✅ **Write prompts that spark great submissions** — the creative satisfaction of a prompt that makes people think and discover (when composing tape prompts)

### FEARS
- ❌ **Admin becoming a chore** — if creating and managing rounds feels like work, they'll stop doing it, and the whole game dies (when the hosting friction accumulates)
- ❌ **Being the only one who can host** — feeling trapped as the sole organizer with no way to share or transfer the responsibility (when they want a break)
- ❌ **Participation dropping and not knowing why** — rounds getting quieter with no visibility into whether it's the prompt, the timing, or the platform's friction (when engagement declines)

---

### Anonymix Promise (Wants):
- **Effortless round creation:** Prompt + deadline + go. Minimal fields, smart defaults, one-screen setup.
- **Glanceable status:** See submission/vote counts at a glance without drilling into admin panels.
- **Prompt-first design:** The prompt is the centerpiece of tape creation — make writing it feel good, not bureaucratic.

### Anonymix Answer (Fears):
- **Minimal admin surface:** No dashboard bloat. Create, manage, done. The product handles the game logic.
- **Transferable admin:** Ability to transfer or share session admin role. Not locked to one person.
- **No guilt mechanics for hosts:** The product doesn't blame the host for low participation. Notifications and reminders are the product's job, not the host's.
